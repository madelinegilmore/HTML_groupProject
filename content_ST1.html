Endangered Species

Endangered species are animals, plants, and microorganisms that are at risk of extinction. They are threatened by habitat loss, climate change, pollution, and overexploitation. Endangered species are listed by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) as vulnerable, endangered, or critically endangered.

The following is a list of some endangered species, click on the species to learn more about them:

  • African elephant
  • Sea Turtle
  • Bengal tiger
  • Black rhino
  • Giant panda

African Elephant

African elephants are endangered primarily due to poaching and habitat loss. Despite international bans, elephants are still hunted for their ivory tusks, which are sold illegally on global markets. This has caused dramatic population declines, especially in parts of Central and West Africa, where enforcement is more difficult.

In addition, expanding agriculture and human settlements are shrinking elephant habitats, leading to increased human-wildlife conflict. As elephants lose access to food and migration routes, they often come into contact with communities, sometimes resulting in harm on both sides. Conservation efforts focus on anti-poaching measures, protected areas, and promoting coexistence between people and wildlife.




Here are some facts about African elephants from the World Wildlife Fund

  • African elephants are the world's largest land mammals, reaching up to three meters in height and weighing up to six tonnes.
  • There are approximately 415,000 remaining in the wild, spread across 37 countries in sub-Saharan Africa.
  • They act as essential "landscape architects" by creating clearings for new plant growth and dispersing large seeds, which promotes natural forest regeneration.
  • Both species face severe declines; the African savanna elephant is endangered (with populations decreasing by at least 60% over 50 years), while the African forest elephant is critically endangered (plummeting by over 86% in 31 years).
  • The main dangers to their survival are poaching for the illegal ivory trade, habitat loss and fragmentation from agriculture and infrastructure, and more frequent droughts caused by climate change.
  • As their natural roaming space shrinks, elephants searching for food and water often damage crops and property, leading to dangerous conflicts and sometimes retaliatory killings.
  • Protective strategies focus on stopping the illegal wildlife trade, monitoring populations, restoring habitats, and implementing coexistence measures—such as installing solar-powered deterrent fences—to safely separate wildlife from local communities

Sea Turtle

Sea turtles are endangered due to a combination of human activities and environmental changes. One of the biggest threats is plastic pollution in the oceans, which turtles often mistake for food. Additionally, fishing nets and bycatch accidentally trap and drown many turtles each year, significantly impacting their populations.

Climate change and coastal development also play a major role in their decline. Rising temperatures affect the gender of hatchlings, while beachfront construction disrupts nesting sites where turtles lay their eggs. Conservation efforts focus on protecting nesting beaches, reducing plastic waste, and creating safer fishing practices to help these ancient creatures survive.




Here are some facts about sea turtles from the World Wildlife Fund

  • Turtles have been on Earth for over 100 million years, surviving since the age of dinosaurs.
  • There are seven species of turtles, and most are endangered or threatened.
  • Sea turtles play an important role in maintaining healthy marine ecosystems.
  • Bycatch in fishing gear is one of the biggest threats, killing hundreds of thousands each year.
  • Plastic pollution is dangerous because turtles often mistake plastic for food.
  • Coastal development and climate change are damaging nesting beaches and affecting hatchling survival.

Bengal Tiger

Bengal tigers are critically endangered, primarily due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-wildlife conflict. Forests where tigers live are being cleared for agriculture, roads, and urban development, fragmenting their territory and making it harder for them to find prey. Poaching for tiger parts used in traditional medicine has also severely reduced their numbers.

Human-tiger conflicts occur when tigers wander into villages or livestock areas, often leading to retaliatory killings. Conservation efforts focus on protecting and connecting tiger habitats, enforcing anti-poaching laws, and raising awareness in local communities to reduce conflicts and ensure the survival of this iconic species.




Here are some facts about Bengal tigers from the Project Endangered Tigers

  • The Bengal tiger is the most populous wild tiger subspecies remaining today, with an estimated population of over 2,633 individuals, accounting for nearly 70% of all wild tigers.
  • They are located mostly in India, which is home to around 2,226 of these tigers, though smaller populations also live in Nepal, Bhutan, and Bangladesh.
  • They are the second largest tiger subspecies (behind the Siberian tiger), with adult males averaging between 400 and 500 pounds.
  • Bengal tigers carry a very rare recessive gene that causes leucism, which results in white coats and blue eyes. However, this mutation is incredibly rare in the wild, with only a 1 in 10,000 chance of occurring naturally.
  • The population living in the Sundarbans mangrove forests lead a highly aquatic lifestyle. They are able to drink salty water, swim across multiple water channels a day, and actively hunt aquatic prey like crabs, water monitors, and turtles.
  • Bengal tigers are incredibly adaptable and can also thrive in extreme mountain environments. They have been spotted on camera traps at altitudes as high as 4,400 meters in the Himalayan mountains of Bhutan.
  • Despite recent conservation successes that have helped their numbers rise, Bengal tigers still face severe threats from habitat loss, fragmentation, and poaching for body parts used in Traditional Chinese Medicine.

Black Rhino

Black rhinos are critically endangered, mainly due to poaching and habitat loss. Their horns are highly valued on the black market, driving illegal hunting that has decimated populations across Africa. Even though conservation programs have helped some numbers recover, poaching remains a major threat.

Habitat loss from agriculture and human settlement further limits their grazing areas, making it harder for black rhinos to survive and reproduce. Conservation efforts focus on anti-poaching patrols, habitat restoration, and translocation programs to create safe populations in protected areas, giving these magnificent animals a chance to recover.




Here are some facts about black rhinos from the World Wildlife Fund

  • Black rhinos have a hooked upper lip, rather than a square one, which allows them to act as "browsers" and easily feed on leaves from bushes and trees.
  • Continuously Growing Horns: They possess two horns that grow continually from their skin throughout their lives, much like human fingernails, with the front horn averaging about 19 inches in length.
  • The black rhino population suffered a devastating 98% drop between 1960 and 1995, but thanks to conservation efforts, their numbers have rebounded to almost 6,500 individuals today.
  • Despite their recent population growth, black rhinos are still classified as critically endangered.
  • Poaching remains the deadliest threat to the species, driven heavily by an illegal international market in Asia where the horns are sought after for traditional medicine and as status symbols.
  • As human footprints expand, rhinos are increasingly confined, leading to high population densities in isolated areas that can cause lower breeding rates and a higher risk of disease transmission.
  • Because black rhinos require large territories to survive, efforts to protect their habitats simultaneously benefit many other species that share the land, such as elephants.
  • They serve as an important source of ecotourism income, which is especially vital for local communities in arid regions of countries like Namibia.
  • Conservation groups and government agencies are combatting wildlife crime by equipping rangers with innovative technologies, including DNA sampling, sniffer dogs, and thermal and infrared cameras to catch and deter poachers.
  • In Namibia, communities have been granted the right to establish "conservancies," empowering local people to manage, govern, and economically benefit from protecting the black rhinos on their communal lands

Giant Panda

Giant pandas were once critically endangered due to habitat loss and low reproduction rates, though conservation efforts have improved their status to vulnerable. Much of their natural bamboo forest habitat in China has been cleared for farming and development, leaving populations isolated and dependent on limited food sources.

Their specialized diet of bamboo and slow breeding cycle make recovery challenging. However, conservation programs, including protected reserves, habitat restoration, and breeding initiatives, have helped increase panda populations. These efforts make the giant panda a well-known symbol of successful wildlife conservation.




Here are some facts about giant pandas from the World Wildlife Fund

  • Giant pandas are classified as vulnerable, with about 1,864 left in the wild.
  • They live in mountain forests in southwest China and rely almost entirely on bamboo for food.
  • Pandas must eat large amounts of bamboo daily (up to 84 pounds) to survive.
  • Habitat loss from roads, railways, and development is fragmenting and isolating populations.
  • Although poaching has decreased, pandas can still be accidentally caught in traps set for other animals.
  • Protecting pandas also helps other species, making them an “umbrella species” for conservation.
  • Conservation efforts include creating protected reserves, though not all pandas live within them.
